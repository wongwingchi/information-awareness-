What Is Information Distortion?

Information distortion refers to the fact that the content, meaning or context of the information is distorted or deformed during the dissemination of information, causing the recipient to obtain inaccurate or misleading information. In the process of using the Internet and social media, we are all likely to encounter information distortion. Information distortion includes not only the intentional spread of false information, but also the unintentional misunderstanding or distortion of information.

Why do people believe wrong information?

You may be wondering why some people still believe this seemingly false information? In fact, the core reason lies in human cognitive biases and social psychology mechanisms.

"People are more willing to accept information that conforms to their preconceived opinions. This phenomenon is called cognitive bias in psychology. Publishers will hope to use people's emotional reactions, especially fear and anger, to touch people's basic emotions and identities to achieve their goals. And In the era of information overload, people are often unable to verify every piece of information in detail. Therefore, if the information comes from an authoritative media, people will most likely lose their ability to identify, especially in a situation where the minority obeys the herd effect of the majority. Under the influence, people lose their critical thinking.

Fake News

Fake news refers to information that is deliberately fabricated but the content is untrue. These messages may be created to influence public opinion, manipulate the political process, or drive traffic to a website.


Characteristics

  • Disguised as a formal news report.
  • Cause strong emotional reactions, such as fear or anger.
  • The purpose is usually political manipulation, financial gain or social influence.

Rumors

Rumors are unconfirmed information, usually based on people’s guesses or conjectures, and spread among people. The hallmark of rumors is that their authenticity is not verified and they are often spread by word of mouth.


Characteristics

  • Spread among individuals through word of mouth or social media.
  • Often involves content that is engaging or has an emotional effect.
  • Lack of reliable sources or supporting evidence.

Disinformation

Unlike misinformation, disinformation is the spread of intentionally false or misleading information. The purpose may be to mislead the public, undermine credibility, cause confusion, or achieve other hidden goals.


Characteristics

  • There is a clear intention to deceive.
  • May mimic the format and style of real information to increase its disguise.
  • Often accompanied by carefully crafted strategies to increase credibility and impact.

Misinformation

Misinformation refers to inaccurate information spread unintentionally. The communicator may believe that the information is true, but the information is distorted due to insufficient verification, misunderstanding, or distortion during the communication process.


Characteristics

  • Lack of intentional intent to deceive.
  • Individuals or organizations that disseminate such information may themselves be victims because they believe in the veracity of the information.
  • The information itself is inaccurate, possibly due to misunderstanding, memory errors, or distortions in the transmission process.

So how to we detect fake news?

With the rampant rise of fake news and misinformation on the internet, we must stay cautious at all times when coming across new information on the internet. So, what are some of the ways that we can prevent ourselves from being subjected to the decisiveness of fake news and such?

Of course, one of the easiest way that we can do this would be to confirm if the sources are true or false, aka fact-checking. According to Zhou et al., they have stated in their research that to detect misinformation on the internet, one of the simplest and straightforward ways is to simply fact-check by ourselves and reevaluate the information we’ve consumed. Not only we can confirm the authenticity of the information from different sources, but we would also be less likely to be gullible to fake news and misinformation.

But how do we actually “fact-check”? Here is a video by John Spencer that simplifies the process of fact-checking.

He mentioned that fact-checking can be done with the “5 C’s principle”.

  1. Context. We should look closely at the details of the articles we read, such as their publication dates and sources.
  2. Credibility. We should always check the credibility of the source we view. For example, we should not be looking at sketchy rumour sites to look for news, and instead head to websites owned by large companies or is widely known to be credible such as BBC News, CNN, the New York Times, etc.
  3. Construction. We should analyze the wording and content of the articles we look at, such as if there are any biases or wordings that may contribute to these biases and lead to us being subjected to warped opinions. We should also look for if the information is a fact or simply an opinion.
  4. Corroboration. We should look at more articles in order to broaden our perspective on the subject matter and find other sources making the same claim. This allows us to check if the subject that we’re looking for is true or false.
  5. Compare. While we get information from credible sources, we can also get our information from other sources that have different ideological or political opinions in order to understand the big picture of the situation.

Thus, by increasing our media literacy and broadening our understanding of fake news, rumours, disinformation and misinformation, we gain a better reason to be responsible for what we share on the internet and not contribute to such.
